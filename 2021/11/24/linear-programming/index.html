Standard Form Linear Programming

  • n variables

  • 1 objective function

  • m constraints

    $\mathbf{Ax=b}$

  • All varibles are Non-negative

Converting to the standard form

$$
\begin{aligned}
&\text { Maximize } \quad 3 x_{1}-2 x_{2}-4\left|x_{3}\right| \\\
&\begin{aligned}
\text { s. t. } \quad -&x_{1}+2 x_{2} \leq-5 \\\
&3 x_{2}-x_{3} \geq 6 \\\
&2 x_{1} + x_{3} =12 \\\
&x_{1}, x_{2} \geq 0
\end{aligned}
\end{aligned}
$$

Problem 1: Unrestricted free variables

Every real number can be viewed as positive part and negative part.
$$
\begin{aligned}
&x_{i}^{+}= \begin{cases}x_{i}, & \text { if } x_{i} \geq 0 \\\
0, & \text { otherwise }\end{cases} \\\
&x_{i}^{-}= \begin{cases}0, & \text { if } x_{i} \geq 0 \\\
-x_{i}, & \text { otherwise }\end{cases}
\end{aligned}
$$
$x_i=x_i^+ - x_i^-$

either $x^+_i$ is 0 or either $x^-_i$ is 0

$|x_i|=x^+_i+x^-_i$

potential problem: quadratic constraint: $x^+_i\cdot x^-_i=0$

Problem 2: inequality

adding slack variable for $\le$ or minus excess variable for $\ge$

Problem 3: minimization

Minimize the negative

result:

(-)Minimize
$$
-3 x_{1} \quad+2 x_{2} \quad+4 x_{3}^{+}+4 x_{3}^{-}
$$
subject to
$$
\begin{array}{rcccc}
&-x_{1} &+2 x_{2} & & &+x_{4} & &=&-5 \\\
& &\quad3 x_{2}&-x_{3}^{+}&+x_{3}^{-} & &-x_{5} &=&6 \\\
&2 x_{1} & &+x_{3}^{+}&-x_{3}^{-} &&& =&12\\\
&x_{1}, &\quad x_{2}, &\quad x_{3}^{+}, &x_{3}^{-},&x_{4}, &\quad x_{5} &\geq &0
\end{array}
$$

Still problematic：

  • Missing the quadratic constraint
  • Dimensionality increase
  • One orginal solution becomes infinity ($x^*=5=6-1=7-2=$…)
  • Maximize $c|x| (c\ge0)$, unable to solve. (maximizing a convex function is impossible)

Geometry of LP

Terminologies

  • Feasible domain/region: $P=\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{R}^n \mid \mathbf{Ax=b}, \mathbf{x>0}\}$

  • Feasible solution: $x \in P \neq \emptyset$

  • Consistent: An LP is consistent $\Leftrightarrow$$P \neq \emptyset$ (Determining this is as hard as solving the LP)

  • Bounded Feasible Domain/Region: $\forall \mathbf{x} \in P, \exists M >0, \quad s.t. \quad ||\mathbf{x}|| \le M$

    Insight: Every solution in the feasible region has limited distance to the origin

  • Bounded LP: $\forall \mathbf{x} \in P, \exists M \in R, \quad s.t.\quad c^T\mathbf{x} \ge M$

    Insight: The feasible region has a lower bound M (Standard LP, minimzation problem)

  • Bounded Feasible Region $\rightarrow$ Bounded LP

    (limited solution length -> limited region space -> must have a lower bound)

  • Bounded LP $\not\rightarrow$ Bounded Feasible Region

    2-D Region: first quadrant

    Obj: $\min\ x_1+x_2$

    Bounded LP because the lower bound is 0

    But obviously unbounded region

  • Optimal solution: $\mathbf{x}^ \in P, \mathbf{c}^T\mathbf{x}^=\underset{x\in P}{\min}\mathbf{c}^T\mathbf{x}$

  • Optimal solution set: $\{\mathbf{x}^ \mid \mathbf{x}^\text{ is an optimal solution}\}$. If $\mathbf{x}^ \in P^$, $\mathbf{x}^*$ solves LP.

Hyperplane

$H=\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{R^n} \mid \mathbf{a^T x=}\beta\}$ is a hyperplane, which has an upper and a lower plane.

The upper $H_U=\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{R^n} \mid \mathbf{a^T x\ge}\beta\}$, the lower $H_L=\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{R^n} \mid \mathbf{a^T x\le}\beta\}$.

  • $\mathbf{a}$ is the normal vector of $H$,

    Proof: $\forall \mathbf{m}, \mathbf{n} \in H$, we have $\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{m}=\beta$, $\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{n}=\beta$. Then we have $\mathbf{a}^T(\mathbf{m-n})=0$. Here $\mathbf{m}$ and $\mathbf{n}$ are two arbitarary points in $H$, so $\mathbf{m-n}$ is an arbitarary vector in $H$.

  • and $\mathbf{a}$ points to the upper.

    Proof: Find any $\mathbf{m} \in H, \mathbf{n} \in H_L \setminus H,$ then $\mathbf{n-m}$ is a vector pointing to the lower half space. Then we have $\mathbf{a^T(n-m)=\underbrace{a^T n}_{<\beta}}{-\underbrace{a^T m}_{=\beta}}<0$, which indicates the angle between the normal vector and the lower half space vector $\mathbf{m-n}$ is over $90^\circ$, which proves that the normal vector is pointing to a opposite half, i.e., upper half.

  • Polyhedral set: set of intersections of finite number of a closed half spaces. If it is non-empty and bounded, it is a polytope. $P=\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbf{R}^n \mid \mathbf{Ax=b}, \mathbf{x>0}\}$ is a polyhedral set.

    Proof: Because $\mathbf{Ax=b}$, the n equality constraints, can be seen as intersections of 2n half spaces, and $\mathbf{x>0}$, are corresponding to m half spaces. Then $P$ is formed by intersection of $2n+m$ closed half spaces, so it is a Pollyhedral set.

If $P \neq \emptyset$ and $\exists \beta \in R$ such that
$$
P \subset H_{L}:=\left\{x \in R^{n} \mid-c^{T} x \leq \beta\right\} \tag{1}
$$
then $\underset{x \in P}{\operatorname{\min}} \mathbf{c}^{\mathbf{T}} \mathbf{x} \geq-\beta$
Moreover, if $x^{} \in P \bigcap H$ then $x^{} \in P^{*}$.

First, $-c^{T} x$ forms a hyper plane. (1) means the feasible region $P$ is in its lower half, which means $\forall x \in P$, $-c^Tx \le \beta$, i.e., $c^Tx \ge -\beta$. Then we know the minimum case also holds, i.e., $\min_{x\in P} c^Tx \ge -\beta$. That is, a lower bound of the objective is $-\beta$.

Then if $P$ has intersction $x^*$ with $-c^Tx$, it must be the optimal solution.

（最小值大于等于$-\beta$，等于$-\beta$一定是最小中的最小了，碰到底了。）
